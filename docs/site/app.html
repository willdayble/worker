The product · What the worker uses every day

An inbox that turns messages into booked, safer work.

This is the part that looks like a normal, friendly app — because the hard problems are handled underneath it. A worker connects a channel, sees their conversations, and moves each one from "just enquired" to "booked and done" with less typing, fewer time-wasters, and a quiet safety net. None of these patterns are novel; the research is clear that this layer is a solved problem to borrow from, not to reinvent.

Onboarding in one sitting

A worker should be productive the first afternoon. Onboarding is: create an account, connect a channel (scan a code or enter a pairing code, depending on the channel), watch recent conversations flow in, and read a plain-language notice about what the AI does and what the app can and can't see. No data migration project, no training course.

Honest by default

The onboarding notice covers both directions of AI use — drafting replies and analysing incoming messages — and states plainly that during the prototype the operator can technically decrypt data while a session is live. Consent is informed, not buried.

Inbox → booking, step by step

Every conversation moves along a simple, neutral pipeline. The worker is always in control; the app just removes friction and forgetting.

Figure 1 — The pipeline. Deliberately generic stages that any service business would recognise. Each contact sits in exactly one stage at a time.

  1. A message arrives. It appears in the inbox as a new enquiry, with the contact created automatically and a draft reply already suggested (the worker can ignore it).
  2. Screening. The worker asks their usual questions; an optional external screening check and an advisory red-flag signal help spot trouble early.
  3. Confirmed. When a time and the details are agreed, the booking is recorded — service, fee, date — in a couple of taps.
  4. Completed or Lost. Afterwards, the worker closes the loop so the system learns what actually happened.

Tagging & the services menu

Workers tag contacts and conversations with a customisable menu — the kinds of work offered, the intent of an enquiry, where in the pipeline things stand. The AI suggests tags from the message, but the worker owns the menu and the final say.

Neutral on the surface, always

Public-facing materials and the data model use general-purpose, neutral terms only. Whatever a worker calls their own services privately, the system's schema and anything an investor or the public sees stays generic. This is a deliberate legal and brand choice, not an accident of wording.

Closing the loop

The single most valuable habit the app builds is recording how each booking ended. Four neutral outcomes capture almost everything worth knowing:

Won

Booked and completed. Service, fee and date logged — the raw material for every later insight.

Lost

Didn't convert, for ordinary reasons. Useful for understanding conversion.

Time-waster

Never going to book. Recognising the pattern protects the worker's attention.

Dangerous

Flagged for safety — advisory, human-set, and decoupled so it survives a ban.

Because outcomes are logged consistently, the cross-user dashboards (built for the test phase, with explicit consent) can surface things no individual worker could see alone: which sources convert, time-to-book, where time-wasters cluster.

Knowing where clients came from

When a new client first makes contact, the app records where they came from — which advert, link, or page — and writes it down once, permanently, at the moment the contact is created. First-touch attribution is a well-worn convention; the only discipline that matters is writing it once and never overwriting it.

Tech info: how first-touch attribution is captured For technical readers

Standard marketing-attribution plumbing: capture UTM parameters into hidden form fields, persist them in a cookie across pages, and write the first-touch value into a write-once field at contact creation. The entry-point is stored per contact and never mutated, so attribution can't drift as a client interacts later.

The disclosed AI assistant

The AI is a collaborator, never an autopilot. For each incoming message it does one structured analysis — is this a booking? what's the intent? any safety red flags? a suggested reply — and then hands everything to the worker. The rules that decide what happens next live in ordinary, predictable code, not the model.

Two hard rules, no exceptions

Never auto-send. Never auto-block. A wrongly-sent message or a false "time-waster" flag has real consequences — a denied booking is lost income for the worker. So the AI is advisory on the way in and collaborative on the way out, every time.

Tech info: one structured call, and why disclosure runs both ways For technical readers

Each inbound message triggers a single schema-constrained model call that always returns valid structured data — booking flag and neutral booking fields, intent tags, service tags, advisory red flags, a suggested reply, and a confidence score. A confidence threshold in deterministic code decides whether to stage a draft or escalate to a human.

  • Neutral schema: field names and values stay general-purpose. The AI extracts generic appointment data; it does not broker a transaction.
  • Disclosure both directions: outbound — any AI-authored text reaching a client is approved by the worker (or clearly labelled as an assistant), to satisfy bot- disclosure laws. Inbound — clients' messages are AI-analysed for tagging and red-flagging, which is automated processing of someone else's sensitive data, so the red flags are advisory only and the privacy notice discloses the inbound analysis too.
  • Cost-aware routing: a fast, cheap model handles routine extraction with a large cached prompt; a stronger model is reserved for drafting and judgement. See Costs.

The safety net

Safety is woven through the whole app, not bolted on. An optional external screening source can be checked at the screening stage; an AI behavioural signal flags concerning patterns as a separate, advisory input; and every dangerous-client flag is human-set, append-only, audit-logged, and — crucially — stored independently of the messaging channel so a ban can never erase it. False positives are recoverable by a human, because a wrong flag has real costs too.
