The centerpiece · How messages actually flow

The chat layer is where this product lives or dies.

Everything else — the CRM, the AI, the pretty dashboards — is well-understood and buildable. The hard, unknowable part is whether a worker can keep talking to clients on WhatsApp without losing the account. This page is honest about that, and explains the test that settles it in two weeks for under $100.

The impossible triangle

The original brief asked for three things from WhatsApp at once. Each sounds reasonable. The research found that you can have any one or two of them, but never all three on a single path.

Figure 1 — The three goals are mutually exclusive in pairs. The research puts the odds of the premise exactly as written being durable past 12 months at 5–15%.

So we keep the goals that matter — a worker who books faster and stays safer — and choose the combination that actually survives: history stays on the worker's own phone (only a recent window syncs into the CRM), a business account on the same number (native-enough), and a sanctioned path plus a second channel so a single ban is never the end. The rest of this page shows the evidence for each trade.

Official vs unofficial WhatsApp — the honest comparison

There are two ways to connect to WhatsApp. They are not "good and better" — they are two completely different risk profiles, and the choice shapes the whole company.

The goal Official Cloud API sanctioned Unofficial libraries against the rules
Import full history Capped: ~6 months of texts, ~2 weeks of media, no group chats, via "Coexistence". Can read the phone's on-device history after pairing.
Look native / consumer No — it becomes a Business account; a personal app can't share the number. Closest to "normal," but the number is visibly automation-driven.
Avoid bans Yes — it's the sanctioned channel. No — detected by fingerprint regardless of behaviour. Typical account lifespan 2–8 weeks; cold numbers banned within ~2 hours.
Appeal a ban Normal business support process. Appeals succeed roughly 1 in 37.
Best used as… The durable primary path, where available. A scoped fallback / kill-test only — never the sole dependency.

Source: docs/research/00_synthesis.md §2–3. The On-Premises API that might once have been a third option is fully shut down (23 Oct 2025), so the Cloud API is the only sanctioned route and its constraints are unavoidable.

Our decision

Official Cloud API + Coexistence is the durable primary. The unofficial path (e.g. Baileys) is kept strictly as a kill-test and regional fallback, isolated, and never something a worker's livelihood depends on alone. And because the platform bans this category of work by policy regardless of technique, the product itself must be neutral, general-purpose tooling — which is true on both paths.

Tech info: Coexistence — the only history path, and its fine print For technical readers

Coexistence lets the worker keep using the WhatsApp Business app on their phone while the Cloud API mirrors the same number. Messages sent from the phone echo into the API (smb_message_echoes), so the worker and the CRM see one unified thread.

  • Syncs into the API: ~180 days of 1:1 text, ~14 days of media, all contacts. Excludes groups, calls, disappearing/view-once, broadcast lists.
  • Requires the WhatsApp Business app (green), v2.24.17+ — not consumer WhatsApp. Switching from consumer to Business is not history-destroying when a backup exists (this corrects an early worry that migration wipes history).
  • The worker's full lifetime history survives on-device and via export — only the API sync is capped. We frame this honestly to workers.
  • Operational fragility: the Business app must be opened every 13 days; uninstalling disconnects the integration; disappearing messages / view-once / live location / edit-revoke are disabled while connected.
  • Regional availability is the load-bearing risk. Commonly excluded: EU/EEA, UK, Australia (+61), Japan, South Korea — and the list shifts. Must be verified for each real user's country. Where unavailable → Telegram or a scoped unofficial fallback.

Tech info: the unofficial libraries, and the supply-chain landmine For technical readers

The unofficial path means self-hosting a library that pretends to be a WhatsApp client:

  • Baileys — pure WebSocket, no browser, the most active option (~469k weekly downloads, MIT). ~60–80 MB RAM/session, ~500 sessions/server. Most flexible, but you own all ban-handling and reconnection.
  • whatsapp-web.js — drives a headless Chromium (~250–400 MB/session, ~50/server, needs browser restarts every 1–2h). Heavier; bans reported almost immediately on connect.
  • Hosted gray-market wrappers (Whapi, GreenAPI, WAHA) offer flat per-number pricing but can read all message content — a hard objection for sensitive users. (GreenAPI is community-flagged as Russia-linked; WAHA self-hosted keeps data on your infra.)
Supply-chain risk is real

In December 2025 a poisoned Baileys fork (lotusbail, ~56k downloads) exfiltrated tokens, messages, contacts and media. Any unofficial dependency must be version-pinned and lockfile-audited or it becomes a breach vector. This is one more reason unofficial is fallback-only.

The 24-hour window — the other hard wall

Even on the safe official path, you can't just message clients whenever you like. WhatsApp only lets a business send free-form messages inside a 24-hour window that the client opens by messaging first. Each new message from the client resets the clock. Outside that window, the business can only send pre-approved template messages.

sequenceDiagram
  participant Client
  participant Worker as Worker (via WorkerApp)
  Client->>Worker: "Hi, are you free Friday?"
  Note over Worker: 24h window OPENS — free replies allowed
  Worker->>Client: Free-form reply (drafted by AI, worker-approved)
  Note over Worker,Client: ...24 hours of silence...
  Note over Worker: Window CLOSED
  Worker--xClient: ✗ Cannot send a free-form re-engagement
  Worker->>Client: ✓ Only a pre-approved TEMPLATE message

Figure 2 — Inside the window, replies are free and natural. Outside it, only approved templates. WorkerApp's UI shows the worker exactly which state they're in.

This is a real constraint on the fantasy of "just text all my clients naturally any time." But it's a manageable one: most of a worker's conversation happens inside live windows (and those replies are free), and the CRM surfaces the window state so nobody is surprised.

Tech info: window pricing and message categories For technical readers

Since the per-message pricing change (1 July 2025):

  • Service (non-template replies inside an open window): free and unlimited since 1 Nov 2024.
  • Utility templates: free inside an open window, paid outside it.
  • Marketing templates: always paid, no volume discount.
  • Authentication templates: always paid (volume-discounted).
  • Free entry-point (click-to-WhatsApp ad / Page CTA): all messages free for 72h.

The cost lever is obvious: keep replies inside the free window and the messaging bill collapses to marketing-only. This is why AI inference, not messaging, is the dominant cost — see the Costs page. All rates are medium-confidence; Meta revises the rate card roughly every 6 months — re-verify the destination-country line before committing.

What "appear as native" really means

The honest answer: on the safe path, the worker's number becomes a WhatsApp Business account — same phone number, same single chat thread the client already knows, the green Business app still on the worker's phone. To the client it looks and behaves like a normal business chat. It is native-enough: nobody has to change their number, and the client keeps chatting in the same thread.

What it is not is an ordinary personal account that looks like a friend texting. That illusion only exists on the unofficial path — and it costs you ban-safety, which is not a trade worth making for a tool people depend on for income and safety. We choose to be straight about this rather than promise something we'd have to walk back.

How bans actually happen

Understanding the ban mechanism is what lets us design around it. There are two families of signal:

Fingerprinting

The platform can tell an unofficial client from a real one by how it talks to the servers — regardless of how careful or low-volume you are. This is why "behave nicely" doesn't save an unofficial account.

Behaviour & reports

High send-rate, low reply ratio, messaging strangers, bulk-identical content — and above all, recipients blocking or reporting you, the single fastest route to a ban.

The scary-sounding "cascade ban" — where banning one account chain-bans the rest — is, for the unofficial path, mostly vendor speculation, not documented. What's real is subtler and we account for both:

  • Monoculture waves: if a whole fleet runs the same library from shared infrastructure, a single platform detection update bans a big chunk at once. It looks like a cascade; it's really everyone sharing one fingerprint.
  • Portfolio pooling (official, and documented): since 7 Oct 2025, the platform enforces at the Business Portfolio level — numbers grouped together share quality and limits, so one bad number can block the whole group from scaling. This is a real single-point-of-failure we design around (see Scaling).
Why decoupling is non-negotiable

On a ban, an unprotected design loses the dangerous-client flags exactly when they're needed most. WorkerApp keeps those flags — and all CRM data — completely separate from the messaging session, so a ban costs a channel, not a worker's safety net.

Telegram — the sanctioned second channel

The simplest insurance against "what if WhatsApp bans us?" is to not depend on WhatsApp alone. Telegram offers a sanctioned, official bot API — no adult-content ban, global reach, and no gray-market risk. It runs behind the very same internal interface as WhatsApp, so to the rest of WorkerApp it's just another channel.

The kill-test — settle the biggest unknown first

The single highest-value thing we can do is not build a grand architecture. It's to run a cheap, brutal experiment that tries to falsify the whole premise before a line of platform code is written. If a throwaway number gets banned in hours, we've learned the most important fact in the business for the price of a coffee — and that's a good outcome.

Concretely: run one throwaway number through an off-the-shelf bridge with human-paced, realistic traffic, and measure two things — how long until it's banned, and how much real history actually comes back. In parallel, stand up a single official Coexistence number to check regional availability and the real sync depth. Write no platform code until the first test passes.

Go / No-Go signals

GO — unofficial as fallback

Throwaway number survives ≥ 4 weeks of realistic, human-paced traffic, history depth meets the need, and numbers/IPs can be isolated.

NO-GO — unofficial

Banned within days; or bans cluster across numbers sharing infrastructure; or appeals fail (expected).

GO — official, reframed

Coexistence available in the user's region and a neutral, policy-compliant brand identity survives platform review.

NO-GO — official

Region unsupported, or no compliant brand identity can pass review.
