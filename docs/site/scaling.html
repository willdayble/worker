10 → 100 → 10k → 100k workers

The two paths break in different places.

"Does it scale?" depends on which path. The official path is slow and bureaucratic but stable; the unofficial path is cheap to start but gets fragile once you have more than a few hundred sessions. Knowing where each one bends is what lets us plan around it.

Both paths at four scales

Read this table as two separate stories. The official column breaks on number-provisioning and bureaucratic friction; the unofficial column breaks on per-session compute and ban waves.

Workers Official path stable, bureaucratic Unofficial path cheap, fragile
10 Trivial. 1–2 shared numbers, lowest tier. Prefer official for stability. Fits one small server (~10 sessions). Fine, but fragile.
100 Clear winner. Within the ~20-number cap if numbers are pooled; mid tiers. 100 dedicated sessions already exceed a safe single-server ceiling; 100 cold numbers raise ban-wave odds.
10k Do not use one number per worker — that blows past the cap by orders of magnitude. Pool shared numbers across multiple business accounts; climb tiers; upgrade throughput. ~20+ servers, sharded and sticky-routed; guaranteed rolling ban waves. Not recommended for production.
100k Realistic only via multiple business accounts / messaging partners, the unlimited tier, top throughput, and an async webhook queue. Infeasible. Hundreds of servers, continuous re-registration of banned numbers, unbounded cascade.

Source: docs/research/00_synthesis.md §7. The shapes matter more than the exact thresholds, which are medium-confidence and platform-dependent.

The per-worker-number ceiling

The most important structural fact for the business model: you cannot give every worker their own dedicated number at scale. A business portfolio is capped at roughly 20 numbers by default. So a naive "one number per worker" design hits a wall in the low tens of workers — and the answer is a shared/pooled-number architecture, planned for from the start rather than discovered in a panic at 50 users.

The portfolio single-point-of-failure

Since October 2025, the official platform enforces quality and limits at the business portfolio level: numbers grouped together share a fate. One number behaving badly can drag down — or block from scaling — every number in the same portfolio. This is a documented, real single-point-of-failure, and the response is architectural: isolate portfolios so a problem in one can't cascade across the whole user base.

What "cascade ban" actually means

The dramatic "one ban chain-bans everyone" story is, for the unofficial path, mostly unproven vendor speculation. The real cascade risks are two undramatic ones: a fleet sharing one library fingerprint getting caught in a single detection wave, and this documented portfolio-pooling on the official side. We design for both rather than for the myth.

What we actually do

Pool, don't dedicate

Shared numbers across multiple business accounts from early on — never one number per worker at scale.

Isolate portfolios

Partition numbers so a bad actor or a quality dip can't sink the whole base.

Two channels

Telegram in parallel means a WhatsApp constraint is a degradation, not an outage. See Chat Layer.

Official as the spine

The unofficial path is fallback-only; nothing the business depends on scales on it.

Tech info: the scaling mechanics For technical readers
  • Tiers ramp 250 → 1K → 10K → 100K → unlimited unique business-initiated conversations per rolling 24h, pooled per portfolio (new numbers inherit the portfolio's highest tier — no per-number warm-up).
  • Throughput is 80 msg/sec per number (20 in Coexistence), rising to 1,000 msg/sec only at the unlimited tier with ≥100K uniques/24h and acceptable quality.
  • At 100k workers, the only realistic official architecture is multiple business accounts / messaging partners behind an async webhook queue (acknowledge in <5s, process downstream).
  • Unofficial compute: lightweight sessions run ~500/server, browser-based ones ~50/server with periodic restarts — and none of it survives the ban waves at scale.
