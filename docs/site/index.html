Investor scoping · Overview

A safe place for independent workers to keep their clients — even if a platform bans them.

WorkerApp is a neutral, privacy-first CRM and messaging tool for independent service providers. They connect WhatsApp (or Telegram), their recent chats flow into a simple inbox, and they turn enquiries into bookings faster and more safely — while their client list, safety flags, and booking history live somewhere a platform ban can never reach.

What it is, in one minute

An independent worker already runs their whole business out of a messaging app. New client messages, asks a few questions, agrees a time, writes it down somewhere, remembers who was trouble last time. It works until the messages pile up — and it falls apart completely the day their phone number gets shut off.

WorkerApp sits beside that messaging app, not on top of it. The worker connects their channel once. Their recent conversations appear in a clean inbox with a contact list, a simple pipeline (enquiry → screening → confirmed → done), and a place to record what each booking was worth and how it ended. A clearly-labelled AI assistant drafts replies and spots bookings — but the worker approves every message; nothing is ever sent or blocked automatically. The client on the other end keeps chatting exactly as before and notices nothing.

No payments — ever

WorkerApp never touches money. That single decision removes the entire risk of being cut off by Stripe, Visa, or Mastercard, the fastest way these tools die.

Ban-proof records

The client list, safety flags, and bookings are stored separately from the messaging session. Lose a number, and you lose a channel — not your business.

Privacy by design

Sensitive data is encrypted at rest. Workers are told plainly what we can and can't see today — and where we're heading. (We don't over-claim. See Infrastructure.)

The win condition

We will know this works when a real worker can do four things through WorkerApp, with real clients:

  1. Book faster. Turn an incoming message into a confirmed appointment without leaving the app.
  2. Prune time-wasters. Spot and close out enquiries that will never convert, so attention goes to the ones that will.
  3. Stay safer. Flag a client as dangerous — and have that flag survive, with the worker, no matter what happens to the phone number.
  4. Know where clients come from. See which advert or page a new client found them through, recorded once and for good.

Success criteria mirror docs/SCOPE.md §5. The prototype is judged on whether a test worker — plus a handful of others — can run a real booking loop end-to-end and a cross-user view surfaces insight (sources, conversion, time-to-book).

The honest reframe

The original idea bundled three promises that sound reasonable together but, on close inspection, cannot all be true at once: import a worker's entire message history, look exactly like an ordinary personal account, and never get banned. Careful research showed that any one of those forces you to give up another.

So we kept the goals — faster and safer bookings, less wasted time, the worker owning their own data — and dropped the impossible parts. That's not a retreat. It's what makes the product real, and it's a stronger story for an investor, because the constraints become the moat.

The three-layer mental model

The one idea to take away

The records are independent of the channel. If a WhatsApp number is banned, the worker loses a way of talking to clients — not their client list, their dangerous-client flags, or their booking history. The channel is swappable behind a single internal interface (WhatsApp → Telegram → Signal later). That decoupling is the product.

We also deliberately invert the usual build order. Instead of building the shared infrastructure first, we de-risk the fragile thing first: prove the chat layer can survive, then build the CRM, and build shared infrastructure last (or never). Building a big shared database before you have users is the worst possible first move here — it's the biggest thing to breach and answers none of the questions that actually kill the company.

Why the constraints are the moat

Compliance

Neutral, general-purpose framing and a hard "no payments" rule keep WorkerApp on the safe side of the laws that sink competitors. Hard to copy without rebuilding from the ground up.

Safety

Dangerous-client flags that survive a ban, advisory (never automated) risk-flagging, and a human in the loop on every action. This is a duty-of-care product, and that shows.

Data sovereignty

The worker's records belong to the worker, encrypted, and decoupled from any platform. A real answer to "what happens when the platform turns on you?"

Tech info: the operating decisions behind the framing For technical readers

Three locked decisions do most of the legal and operational work:

  • Neutral product framing. WorkerApp never advertises specific services, brokers a transaction, or takes a per-booking cut. Those facts are what establish "knowing facilitation" in law; avoiding them keeps the tool on the safe side of decisions like Woodhull v. US.
  • No payment flow, ever. Removes payment-processor and card-network deplatforming exposure in a single decision.
  • Decoupled data. The CRM tables (contacts, deals, tags, flags) carry no dependency on a live messaging session; a banned channel is swapped behind a MessagingProvider interface. See the Technical page.

Encryption is described honestly throughout this site: encryption-at-rest today (defeats stolen-disk/backup theft), with true zero-knowledge / client-side end-to-end encryption on the roadmap. We do not claim "we can't read your data" — because while a session is live, technically we can. See Infrastructure for the honest threat model.
