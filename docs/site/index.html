Investor scoping · Overview

A WhatsApp CRM for independent workers, with the records kept separately from the account.

WorkerChat is a web app for independent service workers. They connect WhatsApp (or Telegram), and their recent chats show up in a simple inbox where they manage contacts, take bookings, and flag clients. Clients keep using WhatsApp as normal. The client list, bookings, and safety flags are stored separately from the messaging account, so if a number gets banned the records don't go with it.

What it is

A lot of independent workers already run their whole business out of a messaging app. A client messages, they ask a few questions, agree a time, write it down somewhere, and try to remember who was trouble last time. That's fine until there are too many messages to keep on top of, or the day the number gets shut off and everything goes with it.

WorkerChat connects to that account once and pulls recent conversations into a web app. There's a contact list, a simple pipeline (enquiry → screening → confirmed → done), and somewhere to record what each booking was worth and how it ended. A clearly-labelled AI assistant can draft replies and pick out bookings, but the worker approves every message — nothing is sent or blocked automatically. On the client's side nothing changes; they're still just messaging on WhatsApp.

No payments

WorkerChat never handles money. That keeps it clear of payment processors like Stripe and the card networks, which routinely cut off this kind of service.

Records survive a ban

The client list, safety flags, and bookings are stored separately from the messaging account. If a number is banned, the worker loses a way of messaging, not their records.

Encrypted, and honest about it

Sensitive data is encrypted at rest. Workers are told plainly what the operator can and can't see today, and what's planned. See Infrastructure.

What success looks like

WorkerChat works if a real worker can do four things with real clients:

  1. Book faster. Turn an incoming message into a confirmed appointment without leaving the app.
  2. Prune time-wasters. Spot and close out enquiries that will never convert, so attention goes to the ones that will.
  3. Stay safer. Flag a client as dangerous — and have that flag survive, with the worker, no matter what happens to the phone number.
  4. Know where clients come from. See which advert or page a new client found them through, recorded once and for good.

Success criteria mirror docs/SCOPE.md §5. The prototype is judged on whether a test worker — plus a handful of others — can run a real booking loop end-to-end and a cross-user view surfaces insight (sources, conversion, time-to-book).

The reframe

The original idea asked for three things at once: import a worker's entire message history, look exactly like an ordinary personal account, and never get banned. The research found you can't have all three — each one forces you to give up another.

So we kept the goals that matter (faster and safer bookings, less wasted time, the worker owning their own data) and dropped the parts that can't work. The constraints that remain are also what makes the thing hard to copy: staying compliant, keeping workers safe, and giving them control of their own data aren't easy to bolt on later.

The main point

The records don't depend on the channel. If a WhatsApp number is banned, the worker loses a way of talking to clients, not their client list, their dangerous-client flags, or their booking history. The channel sits behind a single internal interface, so it can be swapped (WhatsApp → Telegram → Signal later) without touching the records.

We also build in the reverse of the usual order. Rather than building the shared infrastructure first, we start with the part most likely to break: get the chat layer working and surviving, then build the CRM, and leave shared infrastructure until last (or skip it). A big shared database with no users yet is the riskiest thing to build first — it's the largest target for a breach and it answers none of the questions that decide whether this works.

Why the constraints help

Compliance

Neutral framing and a hard "no payments" rule keep WorkerChat clear of the laws that catch out similar tools. That's not easy to copy without rebuilding from scratch.

Safety

Dangerous-client flags that survive a ban, risk-flagging that's advisory rather than automated, and a person approving every action.

Data ownership

The worker's records belong to the worker, are encrypted, and don't depend on any one platform. So there's an answer to "what happens if the platform shuts me out?"

Tech info: the operating decisions behind the framing For technical readers

Three locked decisions do most of the legal and operational work:

  • Neutral product framing. WorkerChat never advertises specific services, brokers a transaction, or takes a per-booking cut. Those facts are what establish "knowing facilitation" in law; avoiding them keeps the tool on the safe side of decisions like Woodhull v. US.
  • No payment flow, ever. Removes payment-processor and card-network deplatforming exposure in a single decision.
  • Decoupled data. The CRM tables (contacts, deals, tags, flags) carry no dependency on a live messaging session; a banned channel is swapped behind a MessagingProvider interface. See the Technical page.

Encryption is described honestly throughout this site: encryption-at-rest today (defeats stolen-disk/backup theft), with true zero-knowledge / client-side end-to-end encryption on the roadmap. We do not claim "we can't read your data" — because while a session is live, technically we can. See Infrastructure for the threat model.
