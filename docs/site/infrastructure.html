The foundation · Where the data lives

The worker's records belong to the worker — and outlive any platform.

This is the layer an investor should care about most, because it's where the legal and safety risk concentrates, and it's where the moat is deepest. The promise is simple: a ban, a stolen backup, or a shut-down account never destroys a worker's client list or safety flags. The detail is where we're scrupulously honest about what we can and can't protect today.

Why this is the gravest liability — and it's solvable

The information a tool like this holds is unusually sensitive: real identities plus conversation logs, in a context where a leak isn't an embarrassment but a physical-safety event — outing, stalking, extortion. Under European data-protection law this is "special-category" data (the most protected kind), and flagging a client as dangerous adds criminal-offence data on top. The potential fines run to tens of millions; the human cost is worse.

The reassuring part

This is a solved engineering problem, not an open research question. The defensible answer — encrypted records over a central database where the operator stores ciphertext and keys are held per-user — exists today with mature, audited tools. The challenge is discipline and honesty, not invention.

Data sovereignty, in plain terms

"Sovereignty" here means a few concrete guarantees a worker can actually feel:

It's yours

Your client list, notes, bookings and flags are your records — not a platform's, and not hostage to your phone number.

It survives a ban

Lose the WhatsApp number and the records are untouched. You reconnect a different channel and carry on.

It's encrypted

Sensitive fields are stored as ciphertext. A stolen disk or backup is useless to a thief.

You can leave

You can export your data in plain form and take it with you. No lock-in, by design.

On-device history — owning what we can't import

As the Chat Layer explains, only a recent window of history can be pulled into the CRM. But the worker's full lifetime history never leaves their phone — it stays in the WhatsApp Business app and can be exported. We treat that as a feature, not a shortfall: less of the most sensitive data centralised on our servers means a smaller breach blast-radius. The CRM holds what it needs to do its job (a working window), and the deep archive stays with its owner.

At-rest vs zero-knowledge — the line we will not blur

This is the most important honesty on the whole site. There are two very different promises a product can make about encryption, and they are not the same thing:

We say this plainly to test users

During the prototype, the operator can technically decrypt a worker's data while that worker's session is live. That is not zero-knowledge, and we never market it as such. Test users are told this in plain language. Over-claiming here would be both a trust failure and a legal one.

Why the gap exists at all: a messaging tool that works while the worker's phone is off needs an always-on server holding the live conversation — and an always-on server, by definition, processes some plaintext to do its job. True "operator-can-never-read" encryption assumes the worker's own device does that work, which breaks the "works while your phone is off" promise. It's a genuine trade-off, not an oversight — so we treat it as a spectrum and let each worker pick their point on it.

Tech info: the encryption boundary for the prototype For technical readers

Sensitive columns are stored as ciphertext using libsodium XChaCha20-Poly1305 with envelope encryption: one random per-user Data Encryption Key (DEK) encrypts the data; the DEK is wrapped and the key material lives in a KMS, loaded into the isolated worker process only while that user's session is connected.

  • Encrypted: message bodies, conversation previews, dangerous-client flag reasons, all outbound payloads and destination identifiers, the messaging session credentials, and all client-identifying free text.
  • Not encrypted (operational): ids, timestamps, channel, status, counts — enough to route and query, minimal personal data.
  • Routing without exposing numbers: where an index on a phone number is needed, a salted HMAC is stored, never the raw number.

Honest threat model: at-rest encryption defeats cold theft of a disk, database, or backup. It does not defend against a live-server compromise or theft of the key-management credentials while a session is active. Not zero-knowledge; that's roadmap.

Underneath the encryption, every database table has Row-Level Security, deny-by-default, keyed to the owning user — defence-in-depth under the encryption, never the confidentiality boundary on its own. No policy is allowed to be a blanket allow-all; the build pipeline fails if one slips in.

Multi-tenant isolation

Many workers share one system, but no worker can ever see another's data. Each worker's records are partitioned and access-controlled at the database level, deny-by-default, so isolation is enforced by the infrastructure rather than trusted to application code. In the prototype we run a handful of test users this way to prove the isolation before any real scale.

Future: cross-worker shared safety tags

One of the most valuable things a community of workers can have is a shared, anonymous warning network — a dangerous client flagged by one worker quietly protecting the next. The data model is designed for this from day one, but it is deliberately not built yet, because it carries real legal and privacy weight and must be done with care.

Designed-for, not built

If and when shared safety tags arrive, they'll follow the established harm-reduction posture: closed access to verified workers, anonymous reporting, explicit no-accuracy disclaimers, member-controlled data, advisory-only, and kept separate from law enforcement by default. Flags are append-only and never silently re-bound to a recycled or reassigned number — a changed identity creates a new record and a human-review task, never an automatic re-accusation.

Tech info: why a recycled number is a safety problem For technical readers

Roughly 66% of recycled phone numbers still resolve to a prior owner, and tens of millions are recycled each year with no warning. Naively re-binding a dangerous-client flag to "whoever has this number now" could either hand a stranger a worker's inbound messages or wrongly accuse an innocent new owner. WorkerApp's rule: dangerous-client flags are append-only, and a changed channel identity on a flagged contact creates a new contact plus a human-review task — never an automatic, silent re-bind.
