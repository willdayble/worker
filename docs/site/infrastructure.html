Where the data lives

The worker's records belong to the worker.

This is where most of the legal and safety risk sits, so it's worth getting right. The aim is straightforward: a ban, a stolen backup, or a shut-down account shouldn't destroy a worker's client list or safety flags. The rest of this page is about what we can and can't protect today, said plainly.

Why the data is the biggest risk

The information a tool like this holds is unusually sensitive: real identities plus conversation logs, in a setting where a leak isn't an embarrassment but a physical-safety event — outing, stalking, extortion. Under European data-protection law this is "special-category" data (the most protected kind), and flagging a client as dangerous adds criminal-offence data on top. Fines run to tens of millions; the human cost is worse.

The good news

This is a known engineering problem, not an open research question. The approach — encrypted records over a central database, where the operator stores ciphertext and keys are held per-user — works today with mature, audited tools. The hard part is discipline and being honest about the limits, not inventing anything new.

Who owns the data

In plain terms, that means a few things a worker can rely on:

It's yours

Your client list, notes, bookings and flags are your records — not a platform's, and not hostage to your phone number.

It survives a ban

Lose the WhatsApp number and the records are untouched. You reconnect a different channel and carry on.

It's encrypted

Sensitive fields are stored as ciphertext. A stolen disk or backup is useless to a thief.

You can leave

You can export your data in plain form and take it with you. No lock-in.

Where the data lives

To be clear about what lives where: from the moment a worker connects, every message — inbound and the worker's replies — is captured and stored in our database (encrypted). That stored copy is what the CRM, search, bookings and AI all run on, and it's what survives a platform ban. The conversation does not just stay inside WhatsApp.

What can't be pulled in is the worker's older back-catalogue — the chats from before they connected. As the Chat Layer explains, only a recent window (≈6 months on the official path) can be imported, so anything older stays in the WhatsApp Business app on the phone and can be exported. We treat that limit as a feature, not a shortfall: keeping the deep pre-connection archive on the worker's own device means less of the most sensitive data is centralised on our servers — a smaller breach blast-radius. The CRM holds what it needs to do its job (the working window plus everything onward); the old archive stays with its owner.

At-rest vs zero-knowledge encryption

This is the part it's most important to be straight about. There are two very different promises a product can make about encryption, and they aren't the same thing:

What we tell test users

During the prototype, the operator can technically decrypt a worker's data while that worker's session is live. That's not zero-knowledge, and we don't describe it as such. Test users are told this in plain language. Claiming more than that would be both a trust problem and a legal one.

Why the gap exists: a messaging tool that works while the worker's phone is off needs an always-on server holding the live conversation, and that server has to process some plaintext to do its job. "Operator-can-never-read" encryption assumes the worker's own device does that work, which breaks the "works while your phone is off" part. It's a real trade-off, not an oversight, so we treat it as a range and let each worker pick where on it they want to be.

Tech info: the encryption boundary for the prototype For technical readers

Sensitive columns are stored as ciphertext using libsodium XChaCha20-Poly1305 with envelope encryption: one random per-user Data Encryption Key (DEK) encrypts the data; the DEK is wrapped and the key material lives in a KMS, loaded into the isolated worker process only while that user's session is connected.

  • Encrypted: message bodies, conversation previews, dangerous-client flag reasons, all outbound payloads and destination identifiers, the messaging session credentials, and all client-identifying free text.
  • Not encrypted (operational): ids, timestamps, channel, status, counts — enough to route and query, minimal personal data.
  • Routing without exposing numbers: where an index on a phone number is needed, a salted HMAC is stored, never the raw number.

Threat model: at-rest encryption defeats cold theft of a disk, database, or backup. It does not defend against a live-server compromise or theft of the key-management credentials while a session is active. Not zero-knowledge; that's roadmap.

Underneath the encryption, every database table has Row-Level Security, deny-by-default, keyed to the owning user — defence-in-depth under the encryption, never the confidentiality boundary on its own. No policy is allowed to be a blanket allow-all; the build pipeline fails if one slips in.

Keeping workers' data separate

Many workers share one system, but no worker can see another's data. Each worker's records are partitioned and access-controlled at the database level, deny-by-default, so the separation is enforced by the database rather than left to application code. In the prototype we run a handful of test users this way to check the separation holds before any real scale.

Later: shared safety tags

One of the more useful things a group of workers can have is a shared, anonymous warning network — a dangerous client flagged by one worker quietly protecting the next. The data model allows for this, but it's deliberately not built yet, because it carries real legal and privacy weight and needs to be done carefully.

Allowed for, not built

If and when shared safety tags arrive, they'll follow the established harm-reduction posture: closed access to verified workers, anonymous reporting, explicit no-accuracy disclaimers, member-controlled data, advisory-only, and kept separate from law enforcement by default. Flags are append-only and never silently re-bound to a recycled or reassigned number — a changed identity creates a new record and a human-review task, never an automatic re-accusation.

Tech info: why a recycled number is a safety problem For technical readers

Roughly 66% of recycled phone numbers still resolve to a prior owner, and tens of millions are recycled each year with no warning. Naively re-binding a dangerous-client flag to "whoever has this number now" could either hand a stranger a worker's inbound messages or wrongly accuse an innocent new owner. WorkerChat's rule: dangerous-client flags are append-only, and a changed channel identity on a flagged contact creates a new contact plus a human-review task — never an automatic, silent re-bind.
